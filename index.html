I'm Samuel Briggs.

I'm a Ph.D. candidate in Nuclear Engineering at the University of Wisconsin - Madison, conducting research on radiation damage effects in Ni- and Fe-based alloys. I'm well-versed in utilizing analytical microscopy techniques to perform advanced microstructural characterization and am passionate about developing materials for next-generation nuclear technologies.

About Me

I grew up in a small town in Oregon and was granted the opportunity to pursue an undergraduate degree in Nuclear Engineering at Oregon State University. In my undergraduate tenure my research interested were focused on computational neutron transport and simulation, and I had the pleasure of serving as an intern at the Pacific Northwest National Laboratory and NuScale Power. A materials science course taken as part of my undergraduate curriculum piqued my interest in alloy development for advanced reactor technologies, which prompted me to pursue an advanced degree under the tutelage of Dr. Todd Allen and Dr. Kumar Sridharan at the University of Wisconsin - Madison. I am currently in the process of completing my dissertation research at Oak Ridge National Laboratory where I am working closely with Dr. Kevin Field to assess the radiation tolerance of advanced Fe-Cr-Al alloys for accident-tolerant fuel cladding applications.

Education

University of Wisconsin - Madison

Ph.D. Candidate in Nuclear Engineering & Engineering Physics • Present

M.S. in Nuclear Engineering & Engineering Physics • May 2013

My graduate studies have found me involved in two primary research projects, both focused on assessing the radiation tolerance of metals for advanced reactor applications. The first project is an investigation of defect structures and radiation-induced segregation phenomena in ion-irradiated Ni-based alloys primarily using transmission electron microscopy (TEM) techniques, including relrod dark field imaging, STEM/EDS, and EELS. The second undertaking involves evaluating structure/property relationships for various compositions of neutron-irradiated Fe-Cr-Al alloys for accident tolerant fuel cladding applications and focuses on determination of the composition and dose dependencies for the formation of dislocation loops and α' phase precipitates using TEM, atom probe tomography (APT) and small angle neutron scattering (SANS) techniques.

Oregon State University

B.S. in Nuclear Engineering • June 2011

My undergraduate research focused on developing neutron transport simulations for various nuclear systems. For my senior project, I modeled the core of the high-temperature gas-cooled reactor (HTGR) design using the SCALE software package in order to determine the axial heat generation profile, which was to be used as an input for the High Temperature Test Facility (HTTF), a one-quarter scale test facility model of the HTGR. I graduated Summa Cum Laude with minors in Mathematics and Chemistry.
